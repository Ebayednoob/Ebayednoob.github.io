Lattice Transformation Theory

This visualization demonstrates the geometric and theoretical physical relationship between a close-packed Triangular (or 2D Toda) lattice and the geometrically frustrated Kagome lattice.

For a static, step-by-step graphical breakdown, see the Transformation Steps Diagram.

For an abstract representation of the compression as a gradient flow, see the Theoretical Gradient Flow Diagram.

Deconstruction: Toda ⟶ Kagome

This process shows the geometric origin of the Kagome lattice. It is a direct substructure of the triangular lattice, formed by systematically removing 1/4 of the atoms and reconnecting the remaining ones.

  1. Start: A stable, close-packed triangular lattice.
  2. Identify: A repeating subset of atoms are targeted.
  3. Remove: The targeted atoms are removed, breaking the original bonds.
  4. Re-form: The remaining atoms form new bonds with their closest neighbors, creating the characteristic corner-sharing triangles of the Kagome lattice.

Compression: Kagome ⟶ Toda

This animation visualizes a theoretical physical process. Under uniform compression, a Kagome structure would become unstable and seek a more dense, lower-energy state, which is the triangular lattice. This is an example of a "gradient flow" towards stability.

  1. Start: A low-density Kagome lattice under pressure.
  2. Void Collapse: The large hexagonal voids become unstable and begin to collapse. The bonds of the Kagome structure break.
  3. Atom Insertion: The "missing" atoms are re-inserted into the collapsing voids.
  4. Re-form: As the new atoms settle, the entire structure re-bonds into the highly stable, close-packed triangular lattice.
